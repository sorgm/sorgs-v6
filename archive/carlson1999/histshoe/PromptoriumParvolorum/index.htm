  • ORGONE. Organum.
     The precise period when the use of the organ was introduced into Britain has not been ascertained; it is supposed to have been first used in France in 757. Compare Ann. Fr. breves; Ann. Francorum; and Eginh. Ann. Pepini; which concur in naming that year as the date of the introduction. Eginhard also mentions the arrival in France of a priest from Venice, who was able to construct organs, in 826; but the instrument does not appear to have been generally used in Western Europe before the Xth cent. At that period Elphegus, Bp. Winchester, constructed an organ, the melodious sounds of which are highly commended in the verses of Wolstan. In the time of Edgar, St. Dunstan, who died 988, caused ï¿½organaï¿½ to be constructed for the church of Glastonbury, according to Joh. Glaston. and in that of Malmesbury, where he bestowed ï¿½organa, ubi per ereas fistulas musicis mensuris elaborates dudum conceptas follig vomit anxius auras.ï¿½ W. Malmesb. Life of Aldhelm, Bp. Shirburn, founder of Malmesbury Abbey. Numerous curious particulars are recorded respecting the use of organs in England, as at St. Albanï¿½s, in Cott. MS. Nero, D. VII.; and Croyland, where there were ï¿½ organa soleunia in introitu eccte.cie supertua situate,ï¿½ as well as smaller organs in the choir. Portable instruments, called frequently regals, were much in use, and representations occur in many illuminations and sculptures. A very curious representation of the organ exists in Eadwineï¿½s Psalter, Trin. Coil. Camb. R. 17 i. and has been copied in Struttï¿½s Horda, I. p1. 33. Organs were imported from Flanders, as appears by the Louth accounts, about the year 1500, Archï¿½ol. x. 91; the price of a pair suitable to be set up in the rood-loft of that noble church being ï¿½13. 6s. 8d. It appears that the usual term, a pair of organs, has reference to the double bellows whereby continuous sound was produced; or, according to Douce, to their being formed with a double row of pipes. See Oï¿½Connorï¿½s curious observations on the early use of organs and psalmody in the Irish church, Hib. Script. iv. 153.

  • PIKE, of a schoo. Liripium, DICC. (liripipium, P.)
    ï¿½A pyke of a scho, or of a staffe, rostrum.ï¿½ CATH. ANG. Lliripipium usually denotes the hood with a long appendage, which, as Knyghton describes it, was twisted around the head; but here it seems to be synonymous with poleine, or cracowe, the proper appellation whereby the singular long-peaked shoe, which was in fashion during the early part of the XVth cent., was known. These terms are supposed to be derived from the fashion having been introduced from Poland, and Cracow, its metropolis, possibly by some of the suite of Anne of Bohemia, Queen of Ric. II. Will. Malmsb. however, states that among the effeminate habits of the times of Rufus, ï¿½usus catceorum cam arcuatis aculeis inventus:ï¿½ the pouleines were also much in vogue in France during the reign of Charles V. and forbidden in 1340 and 1365. The monk of Evesham, in the Life of Rich. II. ed. Hearne, p. 53, relates the indignity that was shown in the diocese of Oxford to the messenger of Abp. Courtenay, in 1384, when he was compelled to eat the prelateï¿½s mandate, seal and all; but in retaliation the Archbishopï¿½s adherents ï¿½sciderunt cracowys de sotularilius ailiquorum de familia Epi. Oxon. et ipsoe cracowis edere cogerunt.ï¿½ In a treatise on the virtues of plants, written about the same time, the seed, or cod, of the Cassia fistula is described as of the ï¿½gretnesse of a saucestre, and shap most lyk ï¿½e pyk of a crakow sho.ï¿½ Arund. MS. 42, f. 60, vo. At the period when the Promptorium was compiled such peaked shoes were worn of an extravagant length, and the fashion was restricted by the statutes of apparel, during the reign of Edw. IV. when the length of ï¿½pykes of shoen or boteuxï¿½ was cut down to two inches. See Parl. Rolls, V. 505, 566; Stat, of Realm. Although no early sumptuary statute is found whereby the use of such shoes was restricted to knights or persons of estate, they are mentioned repeatedly, as if accounted specially a part of knightly equipment. Thus in the description of the comely attire of Sir Degore, it is said, ï¿½His shone was croked as a knighte.ï¿½ v. 700. This Romance is supposed to have been written early in the XIIIth cent. The young Torrent of Portugal is described as craving knighthood from the King of Provens, who bids him engage in a feat of arms, ï¿½and wyn the shone,ï¿½ v. 1117; having acquitted himself manfully, becomes at ï¿½myd-mete,ï¿½ and presents himself at the deis in his squireï¿½s habit, ï¿½withoute couped shone,ï¿½ to claim the guerdon; v. 1193. Compare this passage with Vis, of Piers Ph v. 12,099, where a description occurs of one who comes, as if to a just, after the manner of a knight who comes to be dubbed, to win his gilt spurs, ï¿½or galoches y-couped.ï¿½ ï¿½Milieus, a coppid shoo.ï¿½ ORTUS. Ang.-Sax. cop, apex. A large number of poleine shoes, with the wooden pattens which were warn with them during the XVth cent., in accordance with the fashion represented in the drawing in Cott. MS. Julius E. IV. designated as King John, and given in Shawï¿½s Dresses, were discovered in London, Nov. 1843, and are in the possession of Mr. C. R. Smith, F.S.A.
